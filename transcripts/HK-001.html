Historical Context: June 16, 2019 Protest

The June 16, 2019 protest was one of the largest demonstrations in Hong Kong's history, with organizers claiming nearly 2 million participants (hence the reference to "2 million+1"). This followed the June 9 protest and came after the government's announcement to suspend (but not withdraw) the extradition bill. The demonstration was largely peaceful, with participants marching from Victoria Park to the Legislative Council complex.

Historical Context: Key Escalation Events

July 21 (7/21): The Yuen Long attack, where masked men in white shirts attacked protesters and civilians at the Yuen Long MTR station. Police response was widely criticized as inadequate.

August 31 (8/31): Prince Edward station incident, where police entered the MTR station and used force against passengers, leading to widespread allegations of brutality.

October 1 (10/1): A protester was shot in the chest with live ammunition by police during National Day protests.

PolyU Siege: The November 2019 siege of Hong Kong Polytechnic University, where protesters and police clashed for nearly two weeks, resulting in numerous arrests.

Hong Kong's Limited Democracy

Hong Kong's political system under "One Country, Two Systems" featured several democratic elements that were progressively restricted:

  • Legislative Council (LegCo): Only half the seats were directly elected; the other half came from "functional constituencies" representing business and professional sectors
  • District Councils: More directly democratic but with limited power, later reformed after 2019 to reduce elected seats
  • Chief Executive: Selected by a small election committee, not universal suffrage

The demand for "genuine universal suffrage" was central to both the 2014 and 2019 movements.

Hong Kong Identity Surveys

Public opinion research by the University of Hong Kong tracked identity changes over time:

  • Pre-1997: Most identified as "Hong Konger" or "Hong Kong Chinese"
  • Early 2000s: Brief increase in "Chinese" identity
  • 2010s onward: Sharp rise in exclusive "Hong Konger" identity, especially among youth
  • 2019 peak: Record numbers identifying as "Hong Konger" rather than "Chinese"

This shift paralleled growing tensions with Beijing and was particularly pronounced among younger generations.
